03 / 09 Why this matters what you walk away with

By the end, you can configure, extend, and ship an AI coding agent against your own work.

i.
You move past the chat window.

Most developers have used a chat-based AI tool, but few have configured an agent against their own codebase, written a reusable skill, or integrated an agent into code review. That gap is where the leverage lives, and closing it is what this session is for.

ii.
You stop being locked to one vendor.

Per-seat AI products are moving toward usage-based pricing, and the patterns taught here are vendor-agnostic. The skills you build transfer between Claude, Copilot, Cursor, or whatever ships next quarter without rewriting your work.

iii.
You can answer the enterprise questions.

Data handling, auditability, credential exposure, and prompt injection are the real concerns when an agent touches production. You leave with the architectural patterns that address those risks structurally, not as policy promises.

05 / 09 The shared project Patterson Pets · multiplayer runner

Each attendee builds one piece of the game, and we wire everything together at the end.

The workshop's hands-on work centers on a single project that every attendee contributes to: a real-time multiplayer game styled after the Chrome offline runner, reskinned with pets to fit Patterson Vet and ACT's domain. The infrastructure runs on Cloudflare Durable Objects and WebSockets, provisioned ahead of time so the focus stays on the code.

Each attendee builds one modular piece. The build itself is the lesson: a complex problem broken into modular units of work, with AI coding agents helping each developer complete their own piece.

06 / 09 An optional follow-on monthly league · pets compete on craft

A monthly league where each team's Claude skill solves a real coding challenge, and a Judge Agent scores its work.

For teams that want a recurring reason to refine how they work with AI agents, the workshop includes an optional follow-on league that begins after TechDays. Each team maintains a small GitHub repository containing a single Claude skill with a personality of the team's own design, which is the team's pet.

Once a month, the league publishes a coding challenge as a GitHub repository. Every team forks the challenge and adds their pet to the fork. The Judge Agent then assigns the challenge issue to each pet in turn, the pet completes the work and opens a pull request, and the Judge Agent reviews the PR on correctness, craft, and economy. The entire cycle runs autonomously through GitHub Actions, every decision is reviewable in the PR thread, and the full trail (prompts, tool calls, token spend, and rubric scores) is auditable after the fact.

The league is genuinely optional; the workshop stands on its own. Scoring favors the same craft that good engineering rewards in production, because in production the cost of a poorly engineered agent is denominated in the same tokens that the league uses as currency.

08 / 09 Who should attend audience

The workshop draws curious developers, thoughtful adopters, and the team around them.

Primary

Developers who are curious about AI coding agents but have not yet configured one for their own work. The workshop is built for the moment between "I've used the chat window" and "this is in my daily workflow."

Also a strong fit

Senior engineers who want to shape how their team adopts AI tooling, and anyone who has used AI tools casually but wants to move past chat-window workflows into something more integrated with day-to-day work.

Welcome

Engineering managers planning team adoption. Product managers and designers interested in the workflow side. Anyone planning to contribute to a team's follow-on competition entry, even on the non-technical side.
